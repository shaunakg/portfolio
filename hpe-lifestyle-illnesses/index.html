heart attack

(noun) - an episode of heart disease often brought on by factors such as hypertension and atherosclerosis, characterised by loss of blood flow and damage to the heart muscle or surrounding tissue.

causes

Heart attacks have many causes, and the risk can be increased due to certain lifestyle descisions. Obesity and poor diet can profilerate into fatty buildup on the walls of arteries - causing loss or reduction of blood flow. Type 2 diabetes and smoking can also cause heart attacks due to chemical changes in blood partially blocking the coronary arteries. The risk of a heart attack can be reduced, see Treatments.

health impacts

Heart attacks have huge impacts on your health. If the heart is stopped or its function is compromised, vital oxygen does not get distributed throughout the body. This will cause death of cell tissue in a short time. Arguably the most critical of these organs is the brain. Reduction or cessation of blood flow (also called stroke) to the brain will cause brain damage. If this goes on for too long (approx 3 minutes), it will cause brain death which is irreversible.

signs and symptoms

There are many signs and symptoms that can signal an incoming or in-progress heart attack. Firstly, pain could be experienced in the jaw, left arm, chest and sometimes back. Patients could feel dizzy, light-headed and in a cold sweat. Even if pain is not occurring, there may be tightness in the chest, jaw arm and neck. An unusual but common symptom is a feeling of impending doom. For female patients, gastrointestinal distress can also be an indictor of a heart attack.

treatment

Treatment for heart attacks is usually carried out in a hospital. There are home and commercial-use defibrillators that can restart the heart in the event of ventricular fibrillation but they are usually for emergency use. If a heart attack is suspected, a patient should be transferred to a hospital as quickly as possible. When there, a variety of surgical and medical procedures can be employed to protect the heart. For example, oxygen therapy can help after a heart attack to quickly diffuse oxygen into the blood. Medicines like anticoagulants and beta blockers (blood pressure reduction) can be used to prevent another or for post-attack care. Surgical procedures like the insertion of a coronary stent are common but they depend on the severity of the attack.

Preventative care is widely available and usually easy to accomplish at home. This includes excercise, healthy diet and limitation of recreational drugs (e.g alcohol and caffeine)
